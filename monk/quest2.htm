Monktacus looked down at his feet. His Roman sandals were in tatters. And he still had many miles to go till he was near the last sighting of the troop of monkeys. There is no motorised transport in the high Himalaya and he had been walking the yak trail now for fourteen days. "there's got to be a better way " he mused. At times he wished that he had taken Trumpet on the Quest and could have just sat on his back. But this was foolish. Trumpet was too noble to be treated just as a beast of burden. Anyway Tony Blair would no longer spare him for something apparently so frivolous.
Uncannily, just as he was thinking this, he rounded a turn in the trail and saw the most wondrous sight.
Leo Dickinson the famous adventurer who had ballooned over Everest had left one of his balloons permanently in the village of Namche. The locals were giving it one of its occasional outings. Monktacus scrabbled in his pocket for some Roman coins. In a thrice he had done a deal with the headman of he village. A loan of the balloon for some Roman coins amounting to 5 years wages for the locals. Monktacus had no experience of ballooning, but the Saddhu who was translating for him claimed it was exactly like voyaging the Astral Plane and that he would take charge.


Monktacus paid off the yak drivers and bad them farewell. This was a tender moment for he had bonded quite strongly with the yaks. it reminded him a wee bit of crossing the Alps on the elephants with Hannibal.
 
Monktacus bought some supplies from  the locals. A new pair of shoes, some rice, some tumba, (the Tibetan alcohol) to keep the Saddhu high.
Monktacus threw out the ballast (he knew that much about ballooning) and waited for the balloon to rise. Nothing happened. He turned round and saw the reason. The Saddhu was expecting to bring with him a full size stone representation of Ganeesha!
Monktacus pondered. The Saddhu had been worth his weight in Roman coinage up till now. His bi-linuistic skills had paved the way to where they were. But Monktacus was used to well ordered obedience from his subordinates. The Saddhu was motivated by all sorts of mystic thoughts and urges, mostly culled at random from the Bhagavad-Gita or the Astral Plane.
Monktacus gave the Saddhu a lecture on the physics of hot air ballooning. Monktacus had after all discussed the laws of motion with Ptolemy in his day. Monktacus dozed off. He had not slept in ten days and hoped that the Saddhu could be trusted to fly the balloon for a while. Hardly had he dozed, though than he was awoken by the balloon madly pitching and assailed by an ear splitting roar. Monktacus imagined the worst. The Saddhu had probably communed with Khali. Imagine his surprise however on seeing this.
 
He could hardly believe his eyes.  Just when a hot air balloon had seemed like the epitome of efficiency, he was being taunted by a 'plane. The turbulence from the plane rocked the balloon. A man lesser than Monktacus would undoubtedly have panicked. And as for Monkey Slave, who knows how that timorous creature would have reacted. Monktacus however calmly adjusted the gas flow to the burner and told the Saddhu they would try and follow the plane as it must lead to an airstrip and it appeared to be descending.

 
Sadly Monktacus was to be disappointed. The plane suddenly produced from nacelles under its fuselage a pair of floats and landed on Lake Gokyo. Monktacus wondered what sort of expedition it could be. He did not have long to wait. Out from the plane stepped Lussia's men and the wee fat dyed-blonde haired kilty man from the Coach House Coffee Shop. 
Unknown to Monktacus, this motley group of whom he had some experience had heard that locally to lake Gokyo, there were remarkably "fresh and vibrant " wall hangings made. Through Monktacus' high powered binoculars, he could just make out the hanging that was about to be purchased. It was in the Buddhist tradition, but Monktacus knew that with its remarkable resemblance to the Monkey Go~~d, that the days of Monkey patronising the Coach House were over.

 
 
Meanwhile back in Monkeytown, Monkey sat listlessly toying with the satellite phone. 
"Come on Monkey" said Monkey Girlfriend "life has to go on; you've got a public in the Jungle, and all those patients at your work. Why -  do you realise that while you've not been at the decks, the Monkeytown club scene has embraced "Afro-Teuchter-Garage_Ceilidh? Yes we have no bananas hasn't resounded round the Jungle in weeks". In times past this would have been a dart to the heart of Monkey. But with the twin burden of lack of Monktacus and doubt about his birth family, the evolution of the Monkeytown Club Scene didn't seem quite so important somehow.
"Can't you just enjoy the story Monkey" said Monkey Girlfriend. "Wasn't that funny with the Saddhu and Ganeesha?"
"That just made me miss Trumpet all the more" replied Monkey.
Monkey Girlfriend could see that there was no dealing with it. Monkey badly needed some sort of hard evidence pertaining to his birth family.
 
At this moment, Monktacus was flying over Ama Dablam. He considered it pretty close to the most beautiful mountain he had ever seen. As they flew over the Shipton Col, the Saddhu started to get agitated. He had been in a trance and was still hardly lucid, but he was trying to tell Monktacus something. Once Monktacus had him calmed down enough to talk some Latin, it transpired that the Saddhu thought he had heard some monkey language drifting through the Aether. Since these type of communication have a line of sight limitation, there might be some monkeys nearby. Monktacus scanned the ground with his high powered binoculars. Away to the North, he thought he saw a glint of light. Fortunately the jet-stream was taking them that way.

 
As they passed over the source of the glint, Monktacus' trained military eye detected a building. There was no time to lose and he hauled on the lanyard that spilled hot air from the balloon. Down they plunged at such a rate that even the perpetually myticised Saddhu became quite alert.

 
Monktacus was a newcomer to the art of ballooning but like most military men, he was adapting on the job. It helps to have an eye that is used to judging the flight of a spear or arrow or the interception course of two war chariots. As  the details of the temple became clear the Saddhu got more and more excited. It was clear that this temple was of very special significance. The Saddhu was rambling away in a mixture of Latin, Nepalese and a curious language that is only used on t he Astral Plane.

 
 In the middle of the complex, Monktacus spied a circular structure "a stupa" he muttered. The Saddhu looked happy. Stupas are the beautiful domed structures seen in many Buddhist Temples. On each of the four sides of the main stupa there are a pair of big eyes. These eyes are symbolic of God's all-seeing perspective. There is no nose between the eyes but rather a representation of the number one in the Nepali alphabet, signifying that the single way to enlightenment is through the Buddhist path. 
Above each pair of eyes is another eye, the third eye, signifying the wisdom of looking within. No ears are shown because it is said the Buddha is not interested in hearing prayers in praise of him. 

But for the moment, fascinating though it may be, the religious significance gave to one more terrestrial. The look in the eyes was very very familiar to Monktacus. It was cheeky, it was mischeivous, IT WAS MONKEY! Or at least it contained the very essence of Monkey's cheeky smile.

Monktacus had arrived, by the most mystical and unpredictable route at Swayambhunath:The Monkey Temple.

He had to pause for breath. Swayambhunath is the stuff of monkey folklore. Every child monkey is told of this golden temple where the monkey takes pride of place. Monktacus himself had often wondered about retiring there when soldiering days were done, but he hadn't even been sure till now that it existed.
 
And exist it certainly did. In his day Monktacus bestrode the Forum in Rome and had fought at least once in the Coloseum. But never had he seen such a beautiful building. And the architecture was only the start. The magnificent thing about Swayambhunath was just the amount of monkeys roaming about. They were everywhere. 

 
Some sat serenely as if contemplating the mysteries of the cosmos like the one shown on the right. Others like the cheeky chap on the left blatantly stole food from the various shrines. But neither the monks nor the tourists seeemed to see this as an affront to the various deities.

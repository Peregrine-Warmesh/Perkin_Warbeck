Perkin Warbeck gunned the 1400cc Polo round the last bend in the A83 and got his first glimpse of Tarbert Loch Fyne.

It had to be the most unlikely rock venue ever, with the possible exception of some village halls played by Mike Scott and Runrig.  

But his editor back at the NME had been most insistent.

Listen Warbeck, I have never know a buzz like this one for BrainRockers. I remember the Stones in Hyde Park with the doves and poem and stuff, I saw the Pistols in Scarborough in complete secrecy and I organised The Staceys at Greenock Arts Guild. Never a buzz like this. BrainRockers are gonna be massive and this is your chance to witness the first gig. And all I am asking you to do is drive 600 miles. Look you can go there on full expenses, check into the poshest hotel in town, play a round at Machrihanish.

 Just make sure we have the exclusive.

 We've been given the heads up by a mysterious source who only goes by the name of Dandine, so Melody Maker and Sounds are nowhere on this one.

 Warbeck's gold American Express card was burning a hole in his pocket but he sincerely doubted the abilities of Tarbert to extend it.

He drove past the Victoria Hotel with scarcely a glance.

 

It might be useful later for writing up the local colour. The Columba had been mentioned as having a pleasant ambience, but after waiting 20 minutes to be served Warbeck walked out.

  Stonefield castle it is then he thought.

 

He was warmly greeted by a chap called Alastair and shown into the magnificent ballroom. "This is the life" he thought. As a rock journo he was more used to a Travel Lodge in a major metropolis. Grand country living with fresh seafood was a rare treat. 

A couple of family groups. He could easily stay incognito here.

Suddenly he noticed that one of the family groups was locked in incredibly serious conversation about the Deerhunter. Nothing odd there but it did seem an incredibly adult film for the youth of the two boys. The subject switched to Hendrix and Warbeck's well tuned antenna started twitching. It did not take long for him to realise he had blundered into BrainRockers at their hotel.

Warbeck then had to make up his mind quickly. He could act dumb and grab a lifetime opportunity to eavesdrop on the band "au naturel" or he could introduce himself and grab a lifetime opportunity  to conduct  the first ever interview with BrainRockers.

He pondered. What would Charles Shaar Murray have done?

Off Warbeck went to the bar to ponder some more. 

2 hours later, Warbeck stumbled into the gloaming, 3 pints of Stella down, decision made. He would conduct the interview. Trouble was, the band was gone. Warbeck could just picture his editor's face when he told him except he wouldn't actually tell him.

On the bright side Warbeck was no worse off than when he started. He had just blown a great chance to meet the band. Well that could be rectified. Rumour had it that although the gig was in local night club "Ardyne", the band would be in the Columba eating pan-seared scallops in garlic pre-gig. 

"Time enough" he thought and ordered another Stella.

2 hours later Warbeck strolled into the bar of the Columba. There was a chance he had missed the gig, but this happened to him frequently and he usually just made stuff up. If there were other journos there he would pilfer their notes after plying them with Furstenberg.

"What's the buzz about this gig ?"

he enquired of the completely inefficient proprietrix.

She looked at him blankly. Warbeck could hardly believe that a gig that was the talk of the Groucho club was a secret next door, but nothing about the current Columba owners surprised him. 

Suddenly he heard ear rending feedback, the like of which he hadn't heard since Ted Nugent. 

"Forget it " he reassured the barmaid who was footering with the till and strolled out into the microclimate.

It was 40 yards to "Ardyne". Warbeck's rangy athletic stride made short work of them.
Warbeck whipped out his notebook. "Father and two sons, three way vocals, acoustic and electric axes." he penned. The look is "slightly bohemian.

The guitars were now blazing and Warbeck knew he had missed nothing as this noise would have been heard in Lochgilphead. Warbeck's brief at the NME was alt-country but a staffer had to keep abreast so he had no trouble identifying the song as a Busted cover "Losing You" 

Warbeck had to see the type of people who could kick up such a stink. 

He breezed past security with a mere flick of his NME ID. He was into the venue and the decibels doubled. He could see why. Stacks of Marshall amps from floor to ceiling.

 

 

There were only actually two guitars and one was acoustic, but it was being poured through God's own effects rack. 

The father and sons thing was curious. Warbeck trawled his filing cabinet memory for a precedent. Johnny Cash used to tour with his family. The Man in Black. With his songs of prison and hard living. But in the rock canon, which was clearly where BrainRockers belonged, was there a precedent? But at just this point in Warbeck's reverie, the band blasted into "You Said No " another Busted cover. This was Warbeck's first moment of worry. Were they just a covers band? Now as it turned out Warbeck was to have this fear allayed, but not till the following day when he was allowed into a song-writing session where the nascent "dream on girl" was heard for the first time by anyone

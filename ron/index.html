Ronald George Small


There’s an old family friend of ours who once summed up the essence of Dignity as being about Work, Faith and Home.

Our brother Ronald was, above all else, a very dignified man, and I want to share with you some of the aspects of his life that made him the person he was, starting from where Moraig has left off, and broadening out into these three areas.

Each of us will have personal memories, favourite moments and stories, and we as a family have gone through lots of them over the past few days and weeks. We are particularly grateful to our brother Doug, who has spent hours over the past few months picking out favourite childhood memories from our Dad’s extensive 35mm slide collection and posting them on Facebook, and also for putting together the album you’ve seen this afternoon.


Although much of what I plan to say today is a formal recognition of our brother’s life and work, I will intersperse this with some personal memories and anecdotes, where they seem apt.


Ronald George was born in Maryfield Hospital on the 1st of August 1954, the first of four sons to parents Ramsay and Aileen Small. He was born at an interesting time, his father had just started work as a House Doctor in Residence at Stracathro Hospital, near Brechin, and the family home was a flat in Lochee Road, Dundee. In those days, long before the European Working Directive, his father used to get every third weekend off, and that only started on a Saturday morning. On a rare weekday evening off, he would catch a bus into Brechin, another to Dundee, spend about an hour with his wife and new son, and get back on the bus, as he had to be back on call by midnight.

Childhood days were spent in Lochee Road, Anstruther, and Irvine Ayrshire, during which the young Ronald was joined by his three wee brothers, Douglas, Kenneth and Iain.


Ronald and Douglas were a good team, their crowning childhood achievement must be the morning when, with exhausted parents both asleep, they turned the second floor living room carpet of the Lochee road flat into a paddling pool, pouring bucket after bucket of water through the floorboard cracks onto the ceiling below.. I don’t think the wifie doon stairs was too happy!


Different Swimming Pool


Moving to 46 Monifieth Road Broughty Ferry in 1961, Ronald was educated (or at least they tried) at Eastern School and Grove Academy. His impressive collection of Complimentary O Grades is a testimony to his life long qualities of persistence and perseverance, and it was while trying, again, to gain a Higher English that he put Technical down on his curriculum, simply to fill out the page. This was a life defining moment, as under the guidance and mentorship of Walter Miles Principal teacher in that subject, he had discovered his true aptitude and vocation.

Four years at Dundee College of Further education followed, and after graduating, he began his professional career in Auchterarder.

At this point, of course, he became Mr Small, the Techy teacher.


It’s been interesting to talk to many of you over this past week or two, and to hear our brother referred to as Ron, Ronny, as well as Ronald. Some here may think of him as ‘the heedie’, and of course he was also Dad, Vater, Grandad. and somewhat bizarrely Uncle Orange!

His proper name, of course, is none of these. The name by which he was known to us all in childhood, and still is to some, was Henry. Why? Those avid readers of the Broons will know that Hen Broon is a lanky, skinny, uncoordinated dreep of a young man… Those of you who have seen those adolescent photographs will recognise that if the bunnet fits….then jings and crivens, just wear it.


Before he got too long and skinny

What you may not know is that his full, official nickname was Henry the Parrot. This comes from the occasion of an infamous water fight, when we three boys, Doug, Kenneth and Iain, together with the usual suspects, the neighbourhood ne-er do wells, had extended our war zone beyond the mere horizontal. Our parents had a rare night off, and were at a friends’ house for dinner.

The phone at the friends’ house rings, and Henry-the parrot- Small interrupts the party

‘squawk, Douglas is on the roof’ says he…. Ah well, punishment by yardstick over the backside again.


Following marriage to Sandra, they set up their family home in Maple Road Perth, and with the move came membership of Perth Baptist Church, then situated in a converted Victorian theatre in Tay Street. If work was an area in which, as you will hear, he achieved great things, serving God through testing times has also been a recurring theme for more than 30 years.

Moraig and Fiona


Gaining promoted posts at both Perth Schools, first the High and then the Academy, the family (as now they were with the arrival of Moraig and Fiona) remained in Perth during the years of low church membership….the small church struggling with the duties and financial responsibilities of owning a Listed building.

One day, Ronald and his pastor, Jack Quinn, were up at the church manse, which was situated on Glasgow Road, looked over the Perth Skyline. A pall of smoke was rising from the town centre;

‘wouldn’t it be an answer to prayer if that was the church going up in smoke’ said one to the other

Indeed it was, although as I recall, the Perth Constabulary took a bit of convincing that God had had a hand in it, rather than one of them.

There followed a period of holy homelessness, which ultimately saw the rebirth of Perth Baptist, on the high ground to the west of town. The seeds had been sown by Jack Quinn and were harvested by Ken Jefferson and Charles Young. I know the number of hours that Ronald, along with the rest of the Perth team put into that project, and it’s a lesson to be learned that shortly after completion, with the new church established, and with the promised land in sight, that Ronald was successful in gaining a position as Principal Teacher at Arbroath High School, leaving the Perth fellowship with the job completed.

These were happy years, first in Solway Gardens and latterly Wemyss Crescent Monifieth. Years of camping.. yes I know, Sandra camping…. Caravanning, long summer treks to France and Italy, of family outings through the Larig Gru, climbing Curved Ridge on the Buchaille Etive Mhor, and Sunrise bacon butties at the top of Shehallion.


Curved Ridge

Years of lunchtime teachers volleyball, and weekends in Huntly, Aberdeenshire, one of his favourite places. Ronald spent happy evenings standing by the river Deveron with Simon, his son in law, his brothers, and Freya and Hamish, his grand-children, in whom he delighted. And then there were those legendary Arbroath High School Variety Shows. Santa’s usual mode of transportation, as every child knows, is a sleigh pulled by reindeer. The Arbroath version, however, arrives laden with gifts on the back of the cleaner’s floor polishing machine.

Throughout those years, Ronald’s skill with wood meant a steady stream of bespoke children’s toys, trucks, cots and doll’s houses, played to death by nephews and nieces, ornaments, display cabinets and furniture, and latterly those remote controlled model boats.


Look around the Living room in what is still the epicentre of the Small family in Monifieth Road, and you will find his craftsmanship in every corner.

When they arrived in Monifieth, Ronald and Sandra re-joined the fellowship at Broughty Baptist, where he had been baptised in the early 1970’s, and began what was to be almost 20 years of faithful service. In a manner somewhat reminiscent of the politics of North Korea, he stepped into his father’s shoes, at one time or another serving as deacon, Chairman and Secretary. He also, being a lifelong left-hander, played a mean upside down guitar, in the praise band Accord, who are playing today.


During this time, his professional career flourished. Under the support and guidance of his great friends and Head Teachers, the late John White and Alan Fraser, and building on the foundation of years of work with the Scottish Exam Board, and latterly Her Majesty’s Inspectorate of Schools. He became Deputy Head Teacher at Arbroath, and finally Head Teacher in Montrose. Nae bad for a wee laddy who sat every English Exam he ever did using Dulce et Decorum Est, as his poetry text. No wonder he started as a woodwork teacher.

I think my favourite work related tale from this time was of a school parents evening at Arbroath high. A colleague was ill, so Ronald had a ‘please take’ parents’ night, not an easy task. Having sat a set of parents down, he went through the academic performance of their son, his aptitudes and difficulties, suggesting ways in which he could perform better, and ways in which they, his parents, could help.

‘Mr Small, that’s very helpful, and you’ve summed up our child very well’, said the father, ‘the only problem is, she’s our daughter’.

Sadly, his time as Head teacher in Montrose was to be cut short by illness, a fact that frustrated him, as he felt, as did we all, that he had much to give, and that he had had finally arrived in the role for which he had served such a long and fitting apprenticeship. He was born to be a leader, an encourager, and he trained to have patience, empathy and to see the potential in others. Like his father before him, he wasn’t one to suffer fools gladly, but he seldom judged anyone a fool, and saw no child, or teacher, as being beyond redemption.

And so we come to the end of Ronald’s earthly journey. The same qualities of patience determination and resolve that helped him to bring out the best in generations of pupils and colleagues were evident throughout his final year. His hillwalking buddies will tell you that even in the thickest of hill fog, when you couldn’t see the end of your nose… a remarkable thing in his case… Ronald would always look skyward and declare

‘it’s brightening up mind you’.

So it was in his later days

‘how are you then?’

‘yeh I’m fine, yeh really, I’m fine’.

Ever practical, he ensured that his family were set up in their new home before leaving them. The only blips in this sensible approach were when his post chemotherapy steroid induced spending binges kicked in, often in Gillies’s of Broughty Ferry, as the trees worth of recently purchased solid oak furniture in the new house can prove.

Eventually Ronald left us on Saturday 17th December 2011, having spent his final hours with Sandra and the girls. But I know, as did he, that his story doesn’t end there.

His was a story of Work, and of Home, and of Faith, and, to quote his favourite poet, well lets face it, the only poet he ever read, what happened on a cold winter’s afternoon in Roxburghe House was not the end, but simply a drawing down of blinds.
